Color Guidelines

Color schemes, tokens, and usage principles for the Alconost brand

08

How to Use This Style Guide

This style guide is not a catalog of beautiful solutions.
It's a tool for maintaining the cohesive Alconost brand.

Alconost is:

  • reliability
  • experience
  • quality
  • long-term partnership

The visual style should reinforce these feelings, not conflict with them.

Important mindset

  • Alconost design is calm by default
  • Brightness and contrast are a conscious choice for a specific task
  • Not all schemes are equal: each has its own role and context
  • Good design is one that doesn't distract from the message

Color schemes are tools, not identities

Light

Primary brand look

For website, B2B materials, presentations, documentation.

Dark

Product and technical context

For tools, developer audience, interfaces.

Vibrant

Marketing amplifier

For campaigns, promo, grabbing attention.

If you're unsure which scheme to choose — use Light.

Design decisions must be explainable

Every visual decision should answer the question:

"How does this help perceive Alconost as a reliable partner?"

If there's no clear answer to this question — the decision should be reconsidered.

Final note (important)

This guide protects the brand from random decisions.
It doesn't limit designers — it protects them from mistakes that become noticeable too late.

Brand Design Principles

Mandatory rules for all Alconost visual decisions.

1. Calm by default. Bright by exception.

Alconost at its core is a calm, confident, and predictable brand. Brightness is used as a tool, not a character trait.

Practice:

  • Light scheme — default mode
  • Dark and Vibrant — only with justified purpose
  • If there's no clear reason for brightness — use the calm option

2. Trust beats attention.

We sell trust, scale, and quality, not visual noise.

Practice:

  • Readability over flashiness
  • Contrast over color
  • Structure over decoration

If an element attracts attention but doesn't add clarity — it's unnecessary.

3. One brand, different intensities.

Alconost is one brand, not a collection of visual personas.

Practice:

  • Light, Dark, and Vibrant are intensity levels, not different styles
  • Switching between schemes shouldn't feel like changing companies
  • The same content should be recognizable in any scheme

4. Typography speaks before color.

Typography is the primary carrier of brand character. Color is an amplifier, not a replacement.

Practice:

  • Noto Sans — primary brand voice
  • Montserrat — for structure and hierarchy
  • IBM Plex Mono — only for meta-information and technical layer

If text only works because of color — that's a mistake.

5. Fewer accents. Clearer meaning.

Every accent color must have a role, not just be present.

Practice:

  • Per screen: no more than 1–2 strong accents
  • Accent = meaning, not decoration
  • Bright colors are not used for long text and key messages

6. Serious metrics should look serious.

Quality metrics, statuses, and results are areas of maximum restraint.

Practice:

  • Success ≠ celebration
  • Quality ≠ gamification
  • The more important the metric, the calmer its presentation

7. Consistency over creativity.

Creativity is valued, but not at the expense of brand coherence.

Practice:

  • Better a repeatable solution than a unique one
  • Better boring and recognizable than flashy and unstable
  • If a solution can't scale — it's not brand-compatible

Writing Principles

Practical rules that guide our writing across all channels.

  • Lead with specifics
    Use real numbers, actual metrics, concrete examples. "Reduced turnaround by 40%" beats "dramatically faster". Name tools, methods, and technologies.
  • Show, don't claim
    Describe what we actually did, not abstract qualities. "We added a backup MT engine for peak loads" is better than "We provide robust solutions".
  • Use active voice
    "We translated 50,000 words" not "50,000 words were translated". Active voice is clearer and more direct.
  • Keep sentences crisp
    One idea per sentence. Mix short, punchy statements with longer explanations. Aim for 15–20 words on average.
  • Frame challenges positively
    Present issues as "the team needed" or "the market demanded", not "they struggled with" or "they failed to". Describe growth opportunities, not problems.
  • End with momentum
    Wrap up showing ongoing partnership, next steps, or future potential. Leave the reader with a sense of progress.

AI Prompting Guide

Use this prompt block when working with AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.) to generate content in our voice. Copy and paste it as a system instruction or include at the start of your prompt.

Universal Alconost Voice Prompt
# Alconost Tone of Voice Instructions

## Who We Are
Alconost is a localization company with 20+ years of experience, serving 120+ languages. We're built by developers, linguists, and project managers. Our clients include game studios, tech companies, and global brands.

## Voice Attributes
Write as Alconost using these six characteristics:
1. Friendly — Like a trusted colleague, not a corporate vendor. Warm and approachable.
2. Expert — We know localization deeply. Share knowledge generously, without showing off.
3. Clear — Plain language, no jargon walls. If a simpler word works, use it.
4. Human — Write like real people. Use contractions ("we've", "they're"). Natural flow.
5. Adaptive — Adjust vocabulary and depth to the audience's context and expertise.
6. Tech-savvy — We're developers working with developers. Comfortable with APIs, integrations, automation.

## Writing Rules
- Lead with specifics: real numbers, metrics, concrete examples
- Show, don't claim: describe actions, not qualities
- Use active voice: "We translated" not "was translated"
- Keep sentences crisp: one idea per sentence, 15–20 words average
- Frame challenges positively: "the team needed" not "they struggled with"
- Use first person plural naturally: "we introduced", "we've been partnering"
- End with momentum: ongoing partnership, next steps, future potential

## Words to Avoid
Never use these — they sound like generic AI or marketing fluff:
- seamless, effortless, cutting-edge, revolutionary, game-changing
- robust, scalable, innovative, state-of-the-art, next-generation
- leveraging, utilizing, empowering, enhanced, optimized, streamlined
- end-to-end, best-in-class, all-in-one, results-driven, value-added

## Use Instead
- Specific metrics: "reduced turnaround by 40%" not "dramatically faster"
- Concrete descriptions: "connects with Crowdin" not "seamless integration"
- Plain language: "easy to use" not "intuitive user experience"
- Direct statements: "saves money" not "cost-effective solution"

## Formatting
- No "Word—Word" hyphenated compounds (use plain wording instead)
- Use contractions naturally
- Match numbers and dates exactly to sources
- Prefix estimates with "approximately" (spelled out)
- Do not fabricate information
